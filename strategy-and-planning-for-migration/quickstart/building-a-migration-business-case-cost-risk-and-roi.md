# Building a Migration Business Case: Cost, Risk, and ROI

A shopping cart migration is a business decision, not just a technical project. The best business cases do not rely on vague optimism like “the new platform will be better.” They connect a clear reason for change to measurable outcomes, realistic costs, and risk reduction.

This article helps you build a migration business case that decision-makers trust. It frames cost drivers, identifies where ROI usually comes from, and explains how to quantify risk so the project is funded and planned realistically.

### What a strong migration business case includes

A credible business case answers four questions:

#### 1) Why change platforms now?

The reason should be specific and tied to business pain or growth needs, such as:

* limitations that block merchandising, operations, or marketing
* rising cost or complexity of maintaining the current setup
* performance or reliability constraints
* scaling requirements (catalog size, international expansion, channel growth)

#### 2) What outcomes will improve?

Outcomes should be measurable. Examples:

* improved conversion rate or AOV due to better merchandising and checkout experience
* faster merchandising and content iteration cycles
* reduced operational friction (support, fulfillment, reporting)
* improved SEO and landing page performance stability after structural improvements

#### 3) What will it cost, end-to-end?

Migration cost is more than the service fee. It includes project time, dependencies, and opportunity cost.

#### 4) What risks exist, and how will you control them?

Risk framing is what turns a migration into an investable plan. Decision-makers fund projects that reduce uncertainty, not projects that ignore it.

### The real cost drivers in a migration project

Costs typically come from a combination of these drivers:

#### Platform and ecosystem costs

* platform subscription and transaction fees
* app and integration costs
* ongoing maintenance and staffing changes (agency or internal)

#### Migration scope and complexity

* catalog complexity (variants, categories, attributes, media)
* customer accounts and order history needs
* promotions and discounts behavior
* SEO continuity scope (priority pages, redirects, content intent)
* any non-standard requirements that require custom handling

#### Project overhead and internal time

* stakeholder alignment and approvals
* validation planning and execution
* content review and rebuild work
* training and operational readiness

A business case is more credible when it explicitly includes internal time costs, not just vendor costs.

### Where ROI usually comes from

ROI is rarely “the migration itself.” ROI comes from what the new platform enables and what problems it removes.

#### Revenue uplift (growth enablement)

Common sources include:

* improved storefront performance and merchandising flexibility
* improved conversion through faster page experiences and better buying paths
* better landing-page performance when content and intent are clearer
* easier campaign execution and promotion management

#### Cost reduction (efficiency and maintenance)

Common sources include:

* reduced technical debt and maintenance burden
* fewer “workarounds” that require manual effort
* fewer app stacks required to achieve core functionality
* reduced support friction when data and workflows are cleaner

#### Risk reduction (avoided losses)

Risk reduction is often the most defensible ROI:

* reduced probability of a failed launch
* reduced post-launch traffic loss and revenue disruption
* reduced customer trust issues and support spikes
* reduced operational downtime during peak periods

**Expert insight**: Many business cases fail because they treat risk as a footnote. In reality, migration ROI is often dominated by avoided disruption. A migration that launches smoothly and stabilizes quickly can be worth more than a migration that “saves money” but creates weeks of churn and firefighting.

### How to quantify migration risk in business terms

You do not need perfect precision. You need a reasonable model.

#### Identify the risk categories that matter most

* traffic continuity risk (SEO and landing pages)
* conversion risk (catalog behavior, checkout, promotions)
* operational risk (support workflows, order history usability)
* customer trust risk (accounts, history, perceived stability)

#### Translate risk into impact scenarios

Examples:

* “If traffic drops by X% for Y weeks, revenue impact is …”
* “If promotions behave incorrectly for a launch campaign, revenue impact is …”
* “If support tickets spike by X%, cost and response-time impact is …”

#### Show how you will reduce risk

Risk reduction strategies should be concrete:

* validate representative samples early (best sellers, key promos, priority pages)
* define acceptance criteria and go-live gates
* assign owners for SEO continuity, promotions, content, and support readiness

A business case becomes persuasive when it shows how risk will be managed, not merely acknowledged.

### A simple business case structure you can reuse

#### The headline

One sentence: what you are doing and why now.

#### The problem and constraints

What the current platform prevents or makes too costly.

#### The plan

High-level roadmap: discovery, validation, execution, go-live readiness.

#### The costs

Platform, migration scope, internal effort, and timeline impact.

#### The benefits

Revenue enablement, efficiency gains, and avoided disruption.

#### The risks and controls

Key risks and the validation plan that reduces them.

This structure aligns stakeholders quickly and sets the project up for realistic execution.

### Conclusion

A migration business case is strongest when it connects a clear business reason to a controlled plan. Decision-makers do not need perfection. They need confidence that scope is understood, outcomes are measurable, costs are honest, and risks are actively managed through validation and go-live gates. When you frame ROI around both enablement and avoided disruption, your migration becomes a strategic investment instead of a leap of faith.
