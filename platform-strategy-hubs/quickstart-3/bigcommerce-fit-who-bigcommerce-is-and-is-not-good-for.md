# BigCommerce Fit: Who BigCommerce is (and is not) good for

BigCommerce is often a strong target when a store needs robust catalog capabilities and scalable operations. Fit becomes clearer when you look at catalog complexity, storefront strategy (single storefront vs multi-storefront), and how much your store depends on custom behaviors.

#### BigCommerce is often a strong fit when

* You have a complex catalog with many variant SKUs per product
* You want a platform that supports both variants and customer-driven customizations (modifiers) in a structured way.
* You want flexibility for multiple storefronts or channels with controlled catalog structures (category trees).
* You want product filtering and structured catalog search as part of the platform’s capability set.

#### BigCommerce can be a harder fit when

BigCommerce can still work, but risk goes up if:

* Your store relies on highly bespoke data behaviors that are not clearly defined in terms of variants vs customizations (modifiers)
* You need unlimited combinations tracked as inventory SKUs (BigCommerce distinguishes between variant-driven inventory items and modifier-driven customization).
* Your migration timeline is tight and your project depends on heavy integration work, since API throughput planning becomes a real factor at scale.

#### Practical expectation setting for beginners

BigCommerce is usually a good place to land if you want a catalog that is structured and scalable. Most migration friction comes from:

* Deciding what becomes a tracked variant SKU vs a non-inventory customization (modifier)
* Confirming category assignments reflect real browse paths
* Planning URL continuity and redirects intentionally
